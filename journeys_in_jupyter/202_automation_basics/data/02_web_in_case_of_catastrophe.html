# Define a URL
HTTPS_URL = 'https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail'

# Open the request
r = urllib.request.urlopen(HTTPS_URL)

# Check the status of my request
status_code = r.getcode()
print('The status of my request is {}!\n\n'.format(status_code))

# Convert the raw bytes to text
raw_data = r.read()
text_data = raw_data.decode()

# Do stuff with HTML output
print('Here is the start of our data from {} !\n\n'.format(HTTPS_URL))
print(text_data[:100])

# Close request
r.close()

<p><i><b>Monty Python and the Holy Grail</b></i> is a 1975 British <a href="/wiki/Slapstick" title="Slapstick">slapstick</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> concerning the <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_legend" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian legend">Arthurian legend</a>, written and performed by the <a href="/wiki/Monty_Python" title="Monty Python">Monty Python</a> comedy group (<a href="/wiki/Graham_Chapman" title="Graham Chapman">Graham Chapman</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Cleese" title="John Cleese">John Cleese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Idle" title="Eric Idle">Eric Idle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Jones" title="Terry Jones">Terry Jones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Palin" title="Michael Palin">Michael Palin</a>), and directed by Gilliam and Jones.<sup id="cite_ref-Directors_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Directors-1">[n 1]</a></sup> It was conceived during the <a href="/wiki/Hiatus_(television)" title="Hiatus (television)">hiatus</a> between the third and fourth series of their BBC television series <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python%27s_Flying_Circus" title="Monty Python's Flying Circus">Monty Python's Flying Circus</a></i>.</p>
<p>In contrast to the group's first film, <i><a href="/wiki/And_Now_for_Something_Completely_Different" title="And Now for Something Completely Different">And Now for Something Completely Different</a></i>, a compilation of <a href="/wiki/Sketch_comedy" title="Sketch comedy">sketches</a> from the first two television series, <i>Holy Grail</i> draws on new material. It parodies the legend of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>'s quest to find the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>. Idle used the film as the basis for the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Spamalot" title="Spamalot">Spamalot</a></i> 30 years later.</p>
<p><i>Monty Python and the Holy Grail</i> grossed more than any British film exhibited in the US in 1975. The film received a 97% "Fresh" rating on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> In the US, the film was selected as the second best comedy of all time in the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> special <i><a href="/wiki/Best_in_Film:_The_Greatest_Movies_of_Our_Time" title="Best in Film: The Greatest Movies of Our Time">Best in Film: The Greatest Movies of Our Time</a></i>; in the UK, readers of <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Film" title="Total Film">Total Film</a></i> magazine ranked the film the fifth greatest comedy film of all time, and a similar poll of <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> viewers placed the film sixth (2000).<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-8">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 932 AD, <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and his squire, Patsy, travel throughout <a href="/wiki/Britain_(place_name)" title="Britain (place name)">Britain</a> searching for men to join the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_the_Round_Table" class="mw-redirect" title="Knights of the Round Table">Knights of the Round Table</a>. In Arthur's travels, he recruits <a href="/wiki/Bedivere" title="Bedivere">Sir Bedevere the Wise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancelot" title="Lancelot">Sir Lancelot the Brave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galahad" title="Galahad">Sir Galahad the Pure</a>, and Sir Robin the Not-Quite-So-Brave-as-Sir-Lancelot, along with their squires and Robin's troubadours. Arthur leads the men to <a href="/wiki/Camelot" title="Camelot">Camelot</a>, but decides it is "a silly place" and sets off elsewhere. As they turn away, <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> speaks to them and gives Arthur the task to find the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>.</p>
<p>Arthur and his men search the land for clues to the Grail. They come to a castle occupied by French soldiers who claim to have the Grail but otherwise insult the Englishmen. Arthur and his men come up with a plan to sneak in using a <a href="/wiki/Trojan_Horse" title="Trojan Horse">Trojan Rabbit</a>, but they completely mishandle its execution and are forced away. Arthur decides that the knights should go their separate ways to search for clues to the Grail's location. A modern-day historian being filmed for a documentary and describing the Arthurian legends is abruptly killed by a knight on horseback, triggering a police investigation.</p>
<p>On the knights travels, Arthur and Bedevere attempt to satisfy the strange requests of the dreaded <a href="/wiki/Knights_Who_Say_Ni" title="Knights Who Say Ni">Knights Who Say Ni</a>. Sir Robin avoids a fight with the Three-headed Giant by running away while the heads are arguing. Sir Galahad is led by a grail-shaped beacon to Castle <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">Anthrax</a>, populated by women, but to Galahad's chagrin, he is "rescued" by Lancelot. Sir Lancelot, after finding a note from Swamp Castle believed to be from a lady being forced to marry against her will, rushes the castle and kills nearly the entire wedding party within, only to discover that the note was sent by an effeminate prince.</p>
<p>Arthur and his knights regroup and are joined by other brother Maynard and monk followers. They meet Tim the Enchanter, who directs them to a cave where the location of the Grail is said to be written, but guarded by the deadly <a href="/wiki/Rabbit_of_Caerbannog" title="Rabbit of Caerbannog">Rabbit of Caerbannog</a>. After the Rabbit kills several of the knights, Arthur uses the Holy <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">Hand Grenade</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> provided by Maynard to destroy the creature. Inside, they find the inscription from <a href="/wiki/Joseph_of_Arimathea" title="Joseph of Arimathea">Joseph of Arimathea</a>, directing them to the Castle of Aarrgh. After evading a giant monster, they arrive at the Bridge of Death and must answer three questions from the bridge-keeper to pass. Lancelot answers first and correctly, and passes on. Sirs Robin and Galahad fail to answer correctly and are thrown over the bridge, while Arthur tricks the bridge-keeper, allowing him and Bedevere to pass.</p>
<p>When Arthur and Bedevere finish crossing, they cannot find Lancelot, unaware he was arrested by modern-day policemen investigating the historian's death. Arthur and Bedevere find the Castle of Aarrgh, but discover it is occupied by the French. They amass a large army of knights to assault the castle, when suddenly a large police force shows up, arrests Arthur and Bedevere for the historian's death, and shuts down the film's production.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Fifteen months before the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> visited the set in May 1974,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalin2006174_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalin2006174-9">[8]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Monty_Python" title="Monty Python">Monty Python</a> troupe assembled the first version of the screenplay.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCLocation_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCLocation-10">[9]</a></sup> When half of the resulting material was set in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, and half was set in the present day, the group opted to focus on the Middle Ages, revolving on the legend of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>. By the fourth or fifth version of their screenplay, the story was complete, and the cast joked the fact that the Grail was never retrieved would be "a big let-down ... a great anti-climax".<sup id="cite_ref-BBCLocation_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCLocation-10">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Graham_Chapman" title="Graham Chapman">Graham Chapman</a> said a challenge was incorporating scenes that did not fit the Holy Grail motif.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neither <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Terry_Jones" title="Terry Jones">Terry Jones</a> had directed a film before, and described it as a learning experience in which they would learn to make a film by making an entire full-length film.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> The cast humorously described the novice directing style as employing the level of mutual disrespect always found in Monty Python's work.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCLocation_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCLocation-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film’s initial budget of approximately £200,000 was raised by convincing 10 separate investors to contribute £20,000 apiece. Three of those investors were the rock bands <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a>, who were persuaded to help fund the film by Tony Stratton-Smith, head of Charisma Records (the record label that released Python’s early comedy albums).<sup id="cite_ref-Mental_Floss_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mental_Floss-13">[12]</a></sup> According to Terry Gilliam, the Pythons turned to rock stars like Pink Floyd, Led Zeppelin and <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> for finance as the studios refused to fund the film and rock stars saw it as "a good tax write-off" due to UK <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a> being "as high as 90%" at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGuardian_090302_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGuardian_090302-14">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Monty Python and the Holy Grail</i> was mostly shot on location in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> particularly around <a href="/wiki/Doune_Castle" title="Doune Castle">Doune Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glen_Coe" title="Glen Coe">Glen Coe</a>, and the privately owned <a href="/wiki/Castle_Stalker" title="Castle Stalker">Castle Stalker</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELi2011_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELi2011-16">[15]</a></sup> The many castles seen throughout the film were mainly either Doune Castle shot from different angles or <a href="/wiki/Hanging_miniature" title="Hanging miniature">hanging miniatures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-QuestLocation_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QuestLocation-17">[16]</a></sup> There are several exceptions to this: the very first exterior shot of a castle at the beginning of the film is <a href="/wiki/Kidwelly_Castle" title="Kidwelly Castle">Kidwelly Castle</a> in South Wales, and the single exterior shot of the Swamp Castle during "Tale of Sir Lancelot" is <a href="/wiki/Bodiam_Castle" title="Bodiam Castle">Bodiam Castle</a> in East Sussex;<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> all subsequent shots of the exterior and interior of those scenes were filmed at Doune Castle. Production designer Julian Doyle recounted that his crew constructed walls in the forest near Doune.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Terry Jones later recalled the crew had selected more castles around Scotland for locations, but during the two weeks prior to <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">principal photography</a>, the Scottish Department of the Environment declined permission for use of the castles in its jurisdiction, for fear of damage.<sup id="cite_ref-QuestLocation_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QuestLocation-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the start of "The Tale of Sir Robin", there is a slow camera zoom in on rocky scenery (that in the voice over is described as "the dark forest of Ewing"). This is actually a still photograph of the Gorge at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Buffalo_National_Park" title="Mount Buffalo National Park">Mount Buffalo National Park</a> in Victoria, Australia. Doyle stated in 2000 during an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Hotdog_(magazine)" title="Hotdog (magazine)">Hotdog</a></i> magazine<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> that it was a still image filmed with candles underneath the frame (to give a heat haze). This was a low-cost method of achieving a convincing location effect.</p>
<p>On the DVD audio commentary, Cleese described challenges shooting and editing Castle Anthrax in "The Tale of Sir Galahad", with what he felt the most comedic <a href="/wiki/Take" title="Take">take</a> being unused because an anachronistic coat was visible in it.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Castle Anthrax was also shot in one part of Doune, where costume designer Hazel Pethig advised against <a href="/wiki/Nudity_in_film" title="Nudity in film">nudity</a>, dressing the girls in <a href="/wiki/Shift_(clothing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shift (clothing)">shifts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-QuestLocation_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QuestLocation-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the scene where the knights were combatting the <a href="/wiki/Rabbit_of_Caerbannog" title="Rabbit of Caerbannog">Rabbit of Caerbannog</a>, a real white rabbit was used, switched with <a href="/wiki/Puppet" title="Puppet">puppets</a> for its killings.<sup id="cite_ref-GilliamJones_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GilliamJones-22">[21]</a></sup> It was covered with red liquid to simulate blood, though the rabbit's owner did not want the animal dirty and was kept unaware. The liquid was difficult to remove from the fur.<sup id="cite_ref-GilliamJones_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GilliamJones-22">[21]</a></sup> He also stated that he thought that, had they been more experienced in filmmaking, the crew would have just purchased a rabbit instead. Otherwise, the rabbit himself was unharmed. Also, the rabbit-bite effects were done by special puppetry by both Gilliam and SFX technician John Horton.</p>
<p>As chronicled in <i>The Life of Python</i>, <i>The First 20 Years of Monty Python</i>, and <i>The Pythons' Autobiography</i>, it was revealed that Chapman was suffering from <a href="/wiki/Acrophobia" title="Acrophobia">acrophobia</a>, trembling, and bouts of forgetfulness during filming. These were the results of Chapman's long-standing alcohol addiction, and he decided from that moment on to remain "on an even keel" while the production continued. Nearly three years after <i>Holy Grail</i>, Chapman vowed to quit drinking altogether (which he successfully achieved in December 1977).</p>
<p>Originally the knight characters were going to ride real horses, but after it became clear that the film's small budget precluded real horses, the Pythons decided that their characters would mime horse-riding while their <a href="/wiki/Porter_(carrier)" title="Porter (carrier)">porters</a> trotted behind them banging <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a> shells together. The joke was derived from the old-fashioned <a href="/wiki/Foley_(filmmaking)" title="Foley (filmmaking)">sound effect</a> used by radio shows to convey the sound of hooves clattering. This was later referred to in the German release of the film, which translated the title as <i>Die Ritter der Kokosnuß</i><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> ('The Knights of the Coconut').</p>
<p>The opening credits of the film feature pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> subtitles, which soon turn into an appeal to visit Sweden and see the country's <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">moose</a>. The subtitles are soon stopped, but moose references continue throughout the actual credits until the credits are stopped again and restarted in a different visual style and with references to <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llamas</a>, animals often mentioned in <i>Flying Circus</i>. The subtitles were written by Michael Palin as a way to "entertain the 'captive' audience" at the beginning of the film.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack">Soundtrack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Soundtrack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to several songs written by Python regular Neil Innes, several pieces of music were licensed from <a href="/wiki/De_Wolfe_Music" title="De Wolfe Music">De Wolfe Music</a> Library. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Ice Floe 9", composed by Pierre Arvay. Used during the beginning titles.</li>
<li>"Countrywide",<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> composed by Anthony Mawer. Used during the beginning titles after the first titlers are sacked.</li>
<li>"Homeward Bound", composed by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Stoeckart" title="Jan Stoeckart">Jack Trombey</a>. Used as King Arthur's heroic theme.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Monty Python and the Holy Grail</i> had its theatrical debut in the United Kingdom in 3 April 1975,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalin2006225_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalin2006225-2">[1]</a></sup> followed by a United States release on 27 April 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalin2006231_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalin2006231-3">[2]</a></sup> It was re-released on 14 October 2015 in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film had its television premiere 25 February 1977 on the <i><a href="/wiki/CBS_Late_Movie" class="mw-redirect" title="CBS Late Movie">CBS Late Movie</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCall2013_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCall2013-33">[32]</a></sup> Reportedly, the Pythons were displeased to discover a number of edits were done by the network to reduce use of <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a> and the showing of blood. The troupe pulled back the rights and thereafter had it broadcast in the United States only on <a href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a> and later other channels such as <a href="/wiki/Independent_Film_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent Film Channel">IFC</a>, where it runs uncut.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/DVD_region_code#1" title="DVD region code">Region 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Criterion_Collection" title="The Criterion Collection">The Criterion Collection</a> released a <a href="/wiki/Laserdisc" class="mw-redirect" title="Laserdisc">laserdisc</a> version of the film featuring <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">audio commentary</a> from directors Jones and Gilliam.<sup id="cite_ref-Conrad_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conrad-35">[34]</a></sup> In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Tristar" class="mw-redirect" title="Columbia Tristar">Columbia Tristar</a> published a two-disc Special Edition <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>. Disc One included two <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">commentary</a> tracks featuring Idle, Palin, and Cleese in the first, and Jones and Gilliam in the second, and "Subtitles for People Who Don't Like the Film", consisting of lines taken from <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Part_2" title="Henry IV, Part 2">Henry IV, Part 2</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Conrad_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conrad-35">[34]</a></sup> Disc Two includes <i>Monty Python and the Holy Grail in Lego</i> (also known as <i>Lego Knights</i> or <i>It's Only a Model</i>), a "<a href="/wiki/Brickfilm" title="Brickfilm">brickfilm</a>" version of the "Camelot Song" as sung by <a href="/wiki/Lego" title="Lego">Lego</a> <a href="/wiki/Minifigure" class="mw-redirect" title="Minifigure">minifigures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> It was created by <a href="/wiki/Spite_Your_Face_Productions" title="Spite Your Face Productions">Spite Your Face Productions</a> on commission from the <a href="/wiki/Lego_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Lego Group">Lego Group</a> and Python Pictures. The project was conceived by the original film's respective Producer and Co-Director, John Goldstone and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Gilliam" title="Terry Gilliam">Terry Gilliam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dailyllama_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dailyllama-37">[36]</a></sup> Disc Two also includes two scenes from the film's Japanese dub, literally translated back into English through subtitles. "The Quest for the Holy Grail Locations", hosted by Palin and Jones,<sup id="cite_ref-Galbraith_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galbraith-38">[37]</a></sup> shows places in Scotland used for the setting titled as "England 932 A.D." (as well as the two Pythons purchasing a copy of their own script as a guide). Also included is a who's who page, advertising galleries and sing-alongs.<sup id="cite_ref-Galbraith_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galbraith-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>A limited-edition DVD release additionally included a copy of the screenplay, a limited-edition film cell/senitype, and limited-edition art cards;<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> however, a few of the bonus features from the Extraordinarily Deluxe Edition were omitted. A 35th anniversary edition on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> was released in the US on 6 March 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> Special features include "The Holy Book of Days," a second-screen experience that can be downloaded as an app on an iOS device and played with the Blu-ray to enhance its viewing; lost animation sequences with a new intro from animator Terry Gilliam; outtakes; and extended scenes with Python member and the movie's co-director Terry Jones.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2000, readers of <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Film" title="Total Film">Total Film</a></i> magazine voted <i>Holy Grail</i> the fifth greatest comedy film of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-8">[7]</a></sup> The next Python film, <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python%27s_Life_of_Brian" title="Monty Python's Life of Brian">Life of Brian</a></i>, was ranked first.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-8">[7]</a></sup> A similar poll of <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> viewers in 2005 placed <i>Holy Grail</i> in sixth (with <i>Life of Brian</i> again topping the list).</p>

<p>In 2005, the film spawned a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a>-winning <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> musical smash hit spinoff, <i><a href="/wiki/Spamalot" title="Spamalot">Spamalot</a></i>. Written primarily by Idle, the show has more of an overarching plot and leaves out certain portions of the movie due to difficulties in rendering certain effects on stage. Nonetheless, many of the jokes from the film are present in the show.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, an <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> prime-time special, <i><a href="/wiki/Best_in_Film:_The_Greatest_Movies_of_Our_Time" title="Best in Film: The Greatest Movies of Our Time">Best in Film: The Greatest Movies of Our Time</a></i>, counted down the best films chosen by fans based on results of a poll conducted by ABC and <i><a href="/wiki/People_(American_magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="People (American magazine)">People</a></i>. <i>Holy Grail</i> was selected as the second best comedy after <i><a href="/wiki/Airplane!" title="Airplane!">Airplane!</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2013, the Pythons lost a legal case to <a href="/wiki/Mark_Forstater" title="Mark Forstater">Mark Forstater</a>, the film's producer, over royalties for the derivative work, <i><a href="/wiki/Spamalot" title="Spamalot">Spamalot</a></i>. They owed a combined £800,000 in legal fees and back royalties to Forstater.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> To help cover the cost of these royalties and fees, the group arranged and performed in a stage show, <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python_Live_(mostly)" class="mw-redirect" title="Monty Python Live (mostly)">Monty Python Live (mostly)</a></i>, held at <a href="/wiki/The_O2_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="The O2 (London)">The O2 arena</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in July 2014.</p>
